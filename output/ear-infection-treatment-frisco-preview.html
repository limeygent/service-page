How to Tell If Your Child Has an Ear Infection

Symptoms vary by age. Younger children can't tell you their ear hurts, so watch for behavioral changes. Here's what to look for and what each sign typically means.

Infants (0-12 months)

  • Increased crying and fussiness, especially when lying down
  • Pulling or tugging at one or both ears
  • Refusing to feed (swallowing changes ear pressure)
  • Restless or disrupted sleep
  • Low-grade fever (only about half of ear infections cause fever)

Note: Many babies tug at their ears for self-soothing. If they're eating, sleeping, and behaving normally, ear tugging alone is usually not a concern.

Toddlers (1-3 years)

Highest risk age group
  • Ear tugging with irritability or crying
  • Poor appetite, vomiting, or diarrhea
  • Fever ranging from 100.5 to 104°F
  • Trouble sleeping or general malaise
  • May not be able to say "my ear hurts"

Note: Children aged 6-24 months are at peak risk because their eustachian tubes are shorter, narrower, and more horizontal than older children's.

School-Age (3+ years)

  • Can usually describe ear pain verbally
  • Complaining of ear fullness or "muffled" hearing
  • Headache alongside ear discomfort
  • Low-grade fever
  • Temporary hearing difficulty

Good news: Ear infections become less common as children grow because their eustachian tubes mature and drain more effectively.

What Happens During an Ear Infection Visit

From check-in to treatment plan, the whole visit takes about 20-30 minutes. Here's what to expect so you and your child can feel prepared.

1

Check-In and History

We'll ask when symptoms started, whether your child has had ear infections before, and what you've tried at home. Because Dr. Leung and Dr. Cheng maintain continuity of care (same doctor every visit), they already know your child's history, which helps catch patterns faster.

2

Ear Examination with Otoscope

The doctor looks inside your child's ear with a lighted otoscope to check for fluid, redness, or a bulging eardrum. This takes about 30 seconds per ear and doesn't hurt, though it may be uncomfortable if the ear is already sore.

3

Diagnosis and Explanation

You'll get a clear answer: acute otitis media (active infection behind the eardrum), otitis media with effusion (fluid without active infection), or otitis externa (swimmer's ear). The doctor explains what she sees and why it matters for treatment.

4

Treatment Plan

Based on your child's age, symptoms, and infection type, the doctor will recommend either watchful waiting with pain management (for mild cases in children over 2) or antibiotics (amoxicillin for 7-10 days is first-line). You'll get specific dosing instructions and home comfort measures.

5

Follow-Up Plan

If antibiotics are prescribed, fever should resolve within 48 hours and ear pain within 72 hours. We'll schedule a follow-up if your child has recurrent infections (3+ in 6 months or 4+ in 12 months), which may warrant an ENT referral for ear tubes.

Three Types of Ear Infections in Children

Not all ear infections are the same. The type determines the treatment, so an accurate diagnosis matters.

Acute Otitis Media (AOM)

The most common type. An active infection behind the eardrum.

  • Cause: Bacteria or virus following a cold
  • Signs: Bulging eardrum, pain, fever
  • Treatment: Antibiotics (if indicated) or watch-and-wait
  • Recovery: 2-3 days after treatment begins

5 out of 6 children will have at least one episode by age 3.

Otitis Media with Effusion (OME)

"Glue ear." Fluid trapped behind the eardrum without active infection.

  • Cause: Often persists after an AOM episode resolves
  • Signs: Feeling of fullness, temporary hearing difficulty, usually painless
  • Treatment: Monitoring (antibiotics are not effective for OME)
  • Recovery: Usually resolves within weeks to months

80-90% of children will have OME before school age.

Otitis Externa (Swimmer's Ear)

Infection of the outer ear canal, not behind the eardrum.

  • Cause: Water exposure, pool or bath
  • Signs: Itchy ear canal, pain when ear is tugged, redness, drainage
  • Treatment: Topical antibiotic ear drops (not oral antibiotics)
  • Recovery: 7-10 days with drops

Common in Frisco during summer swim season (May through September).

What Are Ear Tubes?

Tympanostomy tubes are tiny cylinders placed through the eardrum by an ENT surgeon under brief general anesthesia. The procedure takes 10-15 minutes. Most children go home within 1-2 hours and return to school the next day.

  • Tubes allow fluid to drain and air to circulate, preventing the trapped fluid that breeds infection
  • If infections occur with tubes in place, they're treated with topical ear drops only (more effective, fewer side effects than oral antibiotics)
  • Tubes typically fall out on their own after 8-15 months as the eardrum heals

Cook Children's ENT Center in Frisco and Medical City Frisco both offer pediatric ear tube procedures. Dr. Leung coordinates referrals and manages follow-up care through the entire process.

Common Questions About Ear Infections in Children

How do I know if my child has an ear infection or is just teething?

Teething and ear infections can look similar in infants since both cause fussiness and ear pulling. The key difference is fever: ear infections typically cause fever (100.5°F+), while teething rarely does.

  • Fever + ear pulling – likely an ear infection, call us for a same-day visit
  • Refusing to eat or can't sleep – swallowing changes ear pressure, a strong indicator
  • Ear tugging alone, otherwise happy – usually self-soothing, not infection

The otoscope exam takes 30 seconds and gives a definitive answer. When in doubt, call us.

Does my child need antibiotics for every ear infection?

No. The American Academy of Pediatrics recommends a "watch and wait" approach for children over 2 with mild, one-sided infections. About 80% of these resolve on their own within 48-72 hours.

Dr. Leung will recommend antibiotics when they're clinically needed:

  • Children under 2 – younger immune systems need the support
  • Bilateral infections – both ears affected
  • Moderate to severe symptoms – significant pain or distress
  • Fever above 102.2°F – indicates a more aggressive infection

Are ear infections contagious? Can my child go to daycare?

Ear infections themselves are not contagious. However, the cold or respiratory virus that caused the ear infection may be.

Your child can return to daycare or school as soon as:

  • They feel well enough to participate
  • Any fever has resolved for at least 24 hours without fever-reducing medication

My child keeps getting ear infections. When should we consider ear tubes?

Dr. Leung tracks your child's infection history and will recommend an ENT referral when the pattern reaches clinical thresholds:

  • 3 or more infections in 6 months
  • 4 or more infections in 12 months
  • Persistent fluid between episodes, especially with hearing concerns

She coordinates with Cook Children's ENT Center in Frisco and manages follow-up care through the entire process.

Could my child's allergies be causing their ear infections?

Yes, and this is especially common in Frisco. North Texas has year-round allergens that cause chronic inflammation of the eustachian tubes:

  • Mountain cedar (winter) – causes "cedar fever" with flu-like symptoms
  • Oak and grass pollen (spring) – spikes with north winds and dry conditions
  • Ragweed (fall) – major contributor through November

This swelling traps fluid in the middle ear, creating conditions for bacterial growth. If your child has both seasonal allergies and recurring ear infections, Dr. Leung can create an allergy management plan alongside ear infection treatment to help break the cycle.
